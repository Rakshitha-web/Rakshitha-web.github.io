About Me

My life’s mission is to build minds and help young people to discover and reach their full potential in academia as well as life. As an educator my focus revolves around "Social and emotional learning" (SEL) is the process through which children and adults acquire and effectively apply the knowledge, attitudes and skills necessary to understand and manage emotions, set and achieve positive goals, feel and show empathy for others, establish and maintain positive relationships, and make responsible decisions. I have confidence in my voice as well as the need for the message it carries, one of dynamic effective leadership coupled with strong working relationships with diverse populations. As I continue my journey I actively seek opportunities to help guide students as they find their greatest usefulness in their own communities

For the last 18 years I have collected an invaluable amount of knowledge in education and organizational behavior and development. Much of this experience has been gained working in schools often referred to as “alternative” or “innovative learning environments”. In the scope of my experience I have served as a Residential Hall Director and Freshman Conduct officer at a small Catholic institution in South Euclid Ohio. An Intervention Specialist and Contained classroom teacher for the Euclid City School District and consulted with multiple local school districts on youth enrichment programming for remediation and prevention. However, my transformational experience was in the eleventh grade when my mother and father made a decision that would forever change the direction of my life. Up until that point, I had been traveling, along with the rest of my peers, through the public -school system, receiving an education in complacency. I was well rehearsed and therefore successful taking the standardized testing and was on track for graduation, however, I rarely exercised my ability to think freely, and I was satisfied to not stand out muffling my individuality. It is necessary that I begin my testimony here.

After watching a special on sixty minutes about a small faith-based boarding school in Piney woods Mississippi, my parents made a decision to investigate. They were impressed with the mission of the school that promised to educate the hands, hearts, and minds of all its students. The next thing I knew, I was withdrawn from Cleveland Heights-University Heights School District and enrolled in the Piney Woods Country Life School in Jackson, Mississippi. Cleveland Heights Schools and the Piney Woods School were literally worlds apart. Frankly, at the time I could not understand the necessity to separate me from everything and everyone I knew, in order to produce results, I had been told were beyond my capability. This tie in my life where my peers’ identities were not anchored in what labels they wore or neighborhoods they grew up in, and the social hierarchy was based in competitive intellect, cool kids there had a foreign presence, an integrity and direction seldom seen academically in my home theatre. It was at this junction, that my sense- of myself changed and the power of education took a transformation turn in my life. During this time, I was being introduced to learning in a way public schools had denied; a late-comer, but an efficacious student. In this School, I remember being moved beyond words when I heard the bells on campus. At noon each day, when the bells rang, the entire student body and all others on the campus who could hear them stopped whatever they were doing. This daily tradition, to halt every activity and stand-still, was instituted as a way to pay reverence to the slaves who once walked the school grounds, denied any form of formal education and to reflect on our own personal spiritual journeys.

A bittersweet tale at the end of my twelfth-grade year, I stood one-half credit short of the graduation requirements. My two years of Piney Words coursework could not overcome eleven years of deficiency in the former schools. My mother, Dr. Charleyse Pratt felt it would be unacceptable for me to leave the campus and miss the ceremony of graduation, after all, that I had been through. So, I sat in the back of the auditorium under her arm, watching as my peers walked across the stage. I remember hearing her voice telling me not to cry as she asked my roommate to allow me to pose in his cap and gown. My father sat in the car in protest. When I returned to Cleveland and I completed the required half-credit in the Cleveland Municipal School District summer school. The credits transferred and my diploma came later in the mail. My application to attend Fisk University was submitted and I was accepted. One month before classes began. That summer while earning one-half credit to graduate, I can distinctly remember not fitting in with my old friends. Trying to walk the halls with the same pride as I had at piney woods was different when instead of bells the entire student body stopped for metal detectors.

Four years after that experience, on May 3, 2004, I was determined that I would not be found wanting again and I graduated with a Bachelor of Arts Degree, majoring in Political Science. At multiple junctures in my life, I have been confronted with the reality of how disconnected students can feel in their learning communities. I have become driven by a powerful feeling of purpose similar to when I first heard the sound of those Piney Woods bells that reminded me to stop daily and pay reverence. More than ever, I want to reach out to students in ways that extend beyond curriculum becoming a credible witness for them of the good news of the Gospel and providing an example to them in this world, but my efforts are constrained by the boundaries of public education. To me the connection is clear, I want to Teach.

My passion is to demonstrate the transformation that education brings while increasing learner’s confidence, faith, and trust in God. I am committed to instructing learners from all backgrounds so that they will understand the value of education, with all of its rights and privileges, and most importantly its responsibilities, however, I am not just seeking to teach at an institution. My desire is to teach throughout the world. In the process, I have discovered a passion and commitment to work with youth and faculty, helping to support success, teaching good choices and skill development while simultaneously bridging the cultural and institutional gaps that exist in today’s educational state. My passion now is to demonstrate the transformation that education brings on a larger stage. I am committed to developing and implementing communication strategies on equity issues that affect all students while also collecting, analyzing, and sharing data useful to achieving institutional equity goals.
